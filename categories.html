Turtle Categories


There are seven recognized species of sea turtles: Green turtle, Loggerhead turtle, Hawksbill turtle, Leatherback turtle, Olive ridley turtle, Kemp's ridley turtle, and Flatback turtle. Details about these types of turtles are given below.



(1).Green Turtle


The green turtle, scientifically known as Chelonia mydas, is a magnificent sea turtle species that is widely recognized for its distinctive greenish coloration and remarkable life cycle. This marine reptile can be found in tropical and subtropical waters around the world, inhabiting coastal areas, coral reefs, seagrass beds, and even open ocean.Adult green turtles typically measure between three and four feet in length and weigh around 300 to 500 pounds, although some individuals can reach even larger sizes. Their carapace, or shell, is smooth and composed of bony plates, exhibiting shades of olive green or brown, which provide excellent camouflage in their natural habitats. The undersides of their bodies, known as plastrons, are pale yellow or white.These turtles possess a distinctive beak-like mouth, which they use to consume their primary diet of sea grasses and algae. However, they are also known to consume jellyfish, sponges, and other marine invertebrates. Their strong jaws and serrated jaws allow them to effectively bite and tear apart their food.Green turtles are famous for their remarkable migrations, often traveling hundreds or even thousands of miles between their nesting and foraging grounds. Female green turtles return to the same beach where they hatched to lay their eggs. After a lengthy nesting process, where they dig a hole in the sand and deposit their eggs, the females return to the ocean, leaving the eggs to incubate and hatch on their own.Once hatched, the baby turtles, or hatchlings, make a daring journey from the nest to the sea, facing numerous obstacles and predators along the way. Only a small percentage of hatchlings survive to adulthood, making the green turtle's life cycle a remarkable testament to survival and adaptability.Unfortunately, green turtles face numerous threats, including habitat destruction, pollution, entanglement in fishing gear, and poaching for their meat and shells. Conservation efforts, such as protected marine areas and hatchery programs, are crucial to the survival of this extraordinary species and to maintaining the ecological balance of marine ecosystems.Green turtles have been reported nesting on Sri Lanka's southern shores throughout the year with their peak season being January through March. The places with the highest abundance of this species are Kosgoda and Rekawa Beach.




(2).Loggerhead Turtle


Loggerhead turtles (Caretta caretta) are a species of marine turtles known for their large heads and powerful jaws. Here's some information about loggerhead turtles. Loggerhead turtles are one of the largest sea turtle species, with adults reaching an average length of about 3 feet (0.9 meters) and weighing around 250 pounds (113 kilograms). They have a reddish-brown carapace (shell) and a yellowish-brown plastron (underside). Their large heads are equipped with powerful jaws and a beak-like snout. Loggerhead turtles have a wide distribution and can be found in the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans, as well as the Mediterranean Sea. They are known to undertake long-distance migrations, often traveling hundreds or even thousands of miles between feeding and nesting grounds.Loggerhead turtles inhabit a variety of marine habitats, including coastal waters, bays, estuaries, and the open ocean. They are commonly found in areas with abundant food sources, such as seagrass beds, coral reefs, and rocky coastlines.Loggerhead turtles are primarily carnivorous and feed on a variety of marine invertebrates, including crabs, mollusks, jellyfish, and sponges. They have powerful jaws that allow them to crush the hard shells of their prey.Loggerhead turtles reach sexual maturity between the ages of 20 and 30. Females return to their natal beaches to lay their eggs, a behavior known as natal homing. They construct nests in the sand and lay around 100 to 120 eggs per clutch. The incubation period is typically around 60 days, after which the hatchlings emerge and make their way to the ocean.Loggerhead turtles are listed as a threatened species by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). They face numerous threats, including habitat loss, pollution, entanglement in fishing gear, poaching, and climate change. Efforts are being made worldwide to protect their nesting beaches, reduce bycatch in fisheries, and promote conservation measures to ensure their survival.Many countries have enacted legislation and conservation programs to protect loggerhead turtles and their habitats. International agreements, such as the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) and the Convention on Migratory Species (CMS), also aim to safeguard these turtles and promote their conservation.Loggerhead turtles are magnificent creatures that play a vital role in maintaining the health of marine ecosystems. Protecting their habitats and implementing sustainable fishing practices are crucial for their survival and the preservation of marine biodiversity.




(3).Hawksbill Turtle


The hawksbill turtle, also known as Eretmochelys imbricata, is a critically endangered species of sea turtle. Here's some information about hawksbill turtle.Hawksbill turtles are named for their unique beak-like mouth, which resembles a bird's beak. They have a flattened body, a narrow pointed head, and a pair of overlapping scales on their shells that resemble the pattern of overlapping tiles. Their shells can exhibit various colors, including shades of brown, yellow, and orange.Adult hawksbill turtles typically reach a length of about 2 to 3 feet (60 to 90 centimeters) and weigh around 100 to 150 pounds (45 to 68 kilograms). However, some individuals can grow larger.Hawksbill turtles are found in tropical and subtropical waters around the world. They inhabit coral reefs, rocky coastlines, and shallow coastal areas with plenty of sponges, which form a significant part of their diet.The primary food source for hawksbill turtles is sponges, but they are also known to eat other marine invertebrates like jellyfish, sea anemones, and squid. Their narrow pointed beak is well-suited for reaching into crevices and extracting prey from coral reefs.Hawksbill turtles are critically endangered according to the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List. They face numerous threats, including habitat loss, pollution, climate change, accidental capture in fishing gear, and illegal trade for their shell, which is highly valued in some cultures for making jewelry and other decorative items.Hawksbill turtles play a crucial role in maintaining the health of coral reefs. By feeding on sponges, they help control sponge populations, preventing them from overgrowing and smothering the coral. The intricate movements of hawksbill turtles among the reef also aid in dispersing the seeds of various marine plant species.Hawksbill turtles are known to undertake long migrations between their feeding and nesting grounds. Female hawksbill turtles return to the same beach where they hatched to lay their eggs. The nesting season varies depending on the region but often occurs at night. The females dig a hole in the sand, lay their eggs, cover them, and then return to the sea. The eggs hatch after a couple of months, and the hatchlings make their way to the ocean.Efforts are being made worldwide to protect and conserve hawksbill turtles through initiatives such as protected marine areas, hatchery programs, and raising awareness about their importance and the need for conservation.




(4).Leatherback Turtle


The leatherback turtle (Dermochelys coriacea) is the largest living turtle species and is named for its unique shell, which is composed of a leathery skin rather than a hard, bony structure like other turtles. Here are some key facts about leatherback turtles.Leatherback turtles are known for their impressive size. They can reach lengths of up to 6 to 7 feet (1.8 to 2.1 meters) and can weigh between 550 to 2,000 pounds (250 to 900 kilograms). Their carapace (shell) is covered in a thick, leathery skin that has a ridged appearance and is dark gray or black in color.Leatherback turtles have a widespread distribution and are found in both tropical and temperate waters around the world. They are known to undertake long-distance migrations, traveling thousands of miles between feeding and nesting grounds.Leatherback turtles are primarily carnivorous and have a diet consisting mainly of jellyfish. They have specialized adaptations to consume their prey, including sharp, pointed cusps in their mouths and esophagus that help them grip and swallow jellyfish efficiently.Female leatherback turtles come ashore to nest on sandy beaches, typically at night. They use their powerful flippers to dig a nest in the sand where they lay their eggs. After incubation, which usually takes around 60 to 70 days, the hatchlings emerge and make their way to the ocean. Leatherback turtles exhibit a unique temperature-dependent sex determination, where the incubation temperature determines the sex of the hatchlings.Leatherback turtles face various threats that have led to population declines. These threats include habitat loss, egg poaching, accidental capture in fishing gear, pollution, and ingestion of marine debris, particularly plastic bags mistaken for jellyfish. Efforts are underway globally to protect and conserve leatherback turtles through measures such as establishing protected areas, implementing fishing gear modifications, and raising awareness about the importance of conservation.Leatherback turtles are classified as "Vulnerable" by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). This classification signifies that the species faces a high risk of extinction in the wild if significant conservation actions are not taken. Several countries and organizations are actively working to protect leatherback turtles and their nesting sites.The leatherback turtle is a fascinating and important species, playing a vital role in marine ecosystems. Conservation efforts are crucial to ensure their survival and the preservation of their habitats.




(5).Olive Ridley Turtle


The Olive Ridley turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea) is a species of sea turtle that is found primarily in the warm waters of the Pacific and Indian Oceans. It is named after the olive-green color of its shell. Here are some key facts about Olive Ridley turtles.Olive Ridley turtles are relatively small compared to other sea turtle species, reaching an average length of around 2 feet (65-75 cm) and weighing between 75 and 100 pounds (34-45 kg). They have a heart-shaped carapace (top shell) that is typically olive-green in color.Olive Ridley turtles are famous for their synchronized mass nesting, known as arribadas. During an arribada, hundreds or even thousands of females come ashore simultaneously to lay their eggs on sandy beaches. This phenomenon usually occurs at specific nesting sites.Olive Ridley turtles are primarily carnivorous and feed on a variety of marine invertebrates such as crabs, shrimp, and jellyfish. They also consume seaweed and algae occasionally.The Olive Ridley turtle is listed as vulnerable by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). Threats to their population include habitat loss, pollution, accidental capture in fishing nets (known as bycatch), and illegal harvesting of their eggs.After mating at sea, female Olive Ridley turtles return to land to nest. They dig a hole in the sand and lay around 100 to 150 eggs, which they cover and then return to the ocean. The eggs incubate for approximately 50-60 days before the hatchlings emerge and make their way to the sea.Olive Ridley turtles undertake long-distance migrations between foraging grounds and nesting beaches. They can travel thousands of miles to reach their preferred nesting sites.Efforts are being made worldwide to protect Olive Ridley turtles and their nesting habitats, including implementing conservation measures, raising awareness, and promoting sustainable fishing practices to reduce accidental bycatch.




(6).Kemp's Ridley Turtle


The Kemp's Ridley turtle (Lepidochelys kempii) is a species of sea turtle and is the smallest among all the sea turtle species. Here are some key facts about Kemp's Ridley turtles.Kemp's Ridley turtles have an average carapace (shell) length of about 60-70 centimeters (24-28 inches) and weigh around 36-45 kilograms (80-100 pounds). They have a heart-shaped shell that is grayish-green in color. Hatchlings have a dark gray or black carapace.These turtles are primarily found in the Gulf of Mexico and adjacent waters, including the coasts of Texas, Mexico, and occasionally along the southeastern United States. They are known to migrate long distances.Kemp's Ridley turtles have a unique nesting behavior known as arribada, which means "arrival" in Spanish. During this mass nesting event, hundreds or even thousands of females come ashore simultaneously to lay their eggs on a few specific nesting beaches. This phenomenon usually occurs between April and July.Kemp's Ridley turtles face numerous threats, including habitat loss, coastal development, pollution, accidental capture in fishing gear (known as bycatch), and climate change. The decline in their population is also attributed to the historical overharvesting of eggs and turtles for their meat.The Kemp's Ridley turtle is critically endangered, according to the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List. Conservation efforts, such as protection of nesting beaches, nest relocation, and the use of turtle excluder devices (TEDs) in fishing gear, have been implemented to help conserve and recover their population.After nesting, the eggs incubate in the warm sand for about 45-70 days. Once the hatchlings emerge, they instinctively make their way to the sea. They then spend several years in the ocean, feeding on a variety of prey including crabs, jellyfish, and shrimp, before reaching sexual maturity at around 10-15 years of age.




(7).Flatback Turtle


The flatback turtle (Natator depressus) is a species of sea turtle found in the coastal waters of northern Australia and Papua New Guinea. It is named for its relatively flat carapace (shell) compared to other sea turtle species. Here are some key facts about the flatback turtle.Flatback turtles have a distinctive oval-shaped carapace, which is relatively flat and smooth compared to other sea turtle species. The carapace can vary in color from olive green to gray, and it is covered with small scales. They have a relatively small head and strong flippers, which are adapted for swimming in coastal waters.Adult flatback turtles are medium-sized compared to other sea turtle species. They typically measure around 70 to 100 centimeters (28 to 40 inches) in carapace length and weigh between 70 and 90 kilograms (150 to 200 pounds).Flatback turtles have a limited distribution, primarily found in the waters of northern Australia, including the Great Barrier Reef, and in southern Papua New Guinea. They inhabit nearshore and inshore waters, and their nesting sites are predominantly located on sandy beaches in these regions.Flatback turtles are known for their relatively calm and docile nature compared to other sea turtle species. They are primarily solitary creatures, except during the breeding season when females come ashore to nest. They spend most of their time in shallow coastal waters, foraging for food such as jellyfish, sea cucumbers, and mollusks.Female flatback turtles reach sexual maturity at around 30 years of age. They come ashore to nest during the warmer months, between September and February. Unlike other sea turtles, they do not undertake long migrations to nest. Instead, they usually nest on the same beaches where they hatched. The females dig a nest in the sand and lay around 50 to 70 eggs, which incubate for about 50 to 60 days before hatching.The flatback turtle is classified as a vulnerable species by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). Threats to their population include habitat degradation, predation, incidental capture in fishing gear, pollution, and climate change. Efforts are being made to protect their nesting sites and raise awareness about the conservation of these unique sea turtles.

FACTS

Green Turtle

Kosgoda (north of Galle) and Rekawa (near Tangalle).

The green sea turtle is the largest hard-shelled sea turtle. They are unique among sea turtles in that they are herbivores, eating mostly seagrasses and algae. This diet is what gives their fat a greenish color (not their shells), which is where their name comes from. Green turtles are found throughout the world.

Hawksbill Turtle

Pigeon Island National Park

Hawksbills get their name from their unique beak-like mouth, which resembles that of a hawk and is perfect for finding food sources in hard-to-reach cracks and crevices. They are the only species of sea turtle that can survive on a diet consisting mainly of sponges.

Loggerhead Turtle

Galle district and the Hambantota district

Loggerhead turtles are slow growing, long lived animals that do not reach sexual maturity until they are 35 years old. They are found throughout temperate and tropical regions of the Atlantic, Pacific and Indian Oceans, and are the most abundant species of marine turtle found in United States coastal waters.

Olive Ridley Turtle

Trincomalee Beach

The olive ridley gets its name from the olive green color of its heart-shaped shell. The species is among the smallest of the world's sea turtles and is found primarily in the tropical regions of the Pacific, Indian, and Atlantic oceans. Olive ridley turtles are found throughout the world.

Leatherback Turtle

Mirissa Beach

They are the only species of sea turtle that lack scales and a hard shell. They are named for their tough rubbery skin and have existed in their current form since the age of the dinosaurs. Leatherbacks are highly migratory, some swimming over 10,000 miles a year between nesting and foraging grounds.
